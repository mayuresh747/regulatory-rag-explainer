Turning PDFs into clean Markdown that the chunkers can split safely. Docling for digital PDFs, Marker for scanned batches, and a normalization pass before anything reaches the chunker.

01 · Why parsing matters

Bad parsing is silent

A parser that drops a row from a table, fuses two columns of a fee schedule, or misreads a section number does not throw an error. The chunk written downstream looks plausible. The retrieval downstream returns it confidently. The LLM cites it.

This is the most expensive class of bug in a regulatory RAG system because it surfaces only when a regulator opens the cited PDF and finds the number does not match. The defense is choosing a parser that respects document structure, then validating outputs against the source.

02 · Docling

Docling for digital PDFs

The bulk of the corpus (SMC, WAC, RCW, IBC-WA, SPU Standards, most Director's Rules) is digital PDFs. Docling handles these as the primary parser. It exports structured Markdown with section headers, list items, and tables intact.

Why Doclingpicked v1
Table fidelityTableFormer model
Roughly 97% accuracy on complex regulatory tables, including merged cells and multi-level headers. The TableFormer head specifically targets dimensional standards, fee schedules, and similar structured rows.
Structure preservationnative
Outputs Markdown with section headings as ## XX.YY.ZZZ, list items as - A., tables as pipe-separated Markdown. The chunker's regex patterns work directly on this output.
Speedabout 6s / page
Slower than PyMuPDF4LLM (0.12s / page) but the accuracy delta is worth it. The corpus is ingested once per quarter; speed is not the bottleneck.
LicenseMIT
Permissive, no enterprise tier required. Models are downloaded on first use; runs offline thereafter.
03 · OCR fallback

When the PDF is a scan

Several Director's Rules from the 1980s and 1990s are scanned images, not digital text. Docling has integrated OCR that handles the small volume of these. For high-volume scanned batches (rare so far), Marker (built on Surya OCR) is the fallback: 25 pages per second on H100, 90+ languages, lightweight footprint.

Anything that touches OCR carries higher uncertainty than text-extracted PDFs. The chunker flags every OCR-sourced chunk with is_scanned: true and an ocr_confidence score so downstream retrieval can de-prioritize them when the corpus has a digital alternative.

04 · Tables

Tables are first-class

For regulatory questions, tables are where the answer lives. Dimensional standards, fee schedules, parking minimums, setback requirements: most numbers a user actually asks about appear in tables.

Docling extracts tables as separate first-class items. The chunker pulls them out, tags them with content_type: "table", classifies them by content (dimensional, fee_schedule, zoning), and embeds them as Markdown text rather than flattening to prose. The retriever can filter to tables-only when the user asks for numbers.

05 · Normalization

What "normalized" means here

Between Docling's output and the chunker's input, every document passes through a normalization step in src/chunkers/base.py:

Normalization passes
Strip image artifactsregex
Docling sometimes emits <!-- image --> placeholders for figures. These are removed before chunking; they have no embedding value.
Strip zero-width charactersunicode
Zero-width joiners, soft hyphens, BOMs. These break BM25 token matching and add noise to embeddings.
Collapse excess whitespacecleanup
Three or more consecutive newlines collapse to two. Helps the chunker's paragraph-aware regex match consistently.
Minimum chunk size20 tokens
Chunks below 20 tokens after splitting are garbage (stubs, image-only pages, stray headers) and get dropped. The threshold is set in MIN_CHUNK_TOKENS.
