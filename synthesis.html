Synthesis

The last step. The LLM reads what retrieval pulled in and writes an answer with strict citations. Same model across every arm; the difference is the context it gets.

GPT-5.1 · synthesis·GPT-4.1 mini · query parse·strict cite-or-refuse
01 · Synthesis in context

The smallest differentiator

Across every retrieval arm in the benchmark the synthesis model is the same. KG-RAG, Naive RAG, and ChatGPT all use GPT-5.1. The visible quality difference is determined entirely by what arrives in the context window: how many chunks, how well-cited, with what cross-references attached.

That said, synthesis is not free. The wrong prompt template lets a confident model paper over thin retrieval. The right prompt forces the model to cite or refuse, which surfaces upstream failures instead of hiding them.

02 · Model choices

Two models, two jobs

Query understanding and answer synthesis have different cost-accuracy curves. We split them.

Which model, whenv1 stack
GPT-4.1 miniquery parse + decomposition
Used for intent classification, decomposition into sub-queries, and premise-claim detection. About 2x faster than GPT-5 mini at this task. The work is high-volume and deterministic; small model is the right fit.
GPT-5.1final synthesis
Used for the final answer. Stronger reasoning on conflict adjudication, precedent weighing, and cross-agency interpretation. Run once per question, low volume, high stakes.
Claude Opusjudge only
Not in the user-facing path. Used by the benchmark grader at temperature 0 with a strict rubric to score answer faithfulness and correctness. Independent third opinion.
03 · The strict prompt

Cite what you used. Refuse if silent.

The synthesis prompt is short and rigid. It tells the model what its inputs are, what its output shape is, and what counts as a refusal. The full text is in section 06 below, clickable to see each part.

The non-negotiables are: every cited section must appear in the retrieved chunks; no fabricated section numbers; if the corpus is silent on the question, say so and stop; if the question contains a false premise, correct the premise first and answer the underlying question second.

04 · When to refuse

Three refusal patterns

The most dangerous answer in a regulatory context is the confident wrong one. The prompt explicitly teaches the model when not to answer:

Refusal patterns
Out of scopeno answer in corpus
The question is in-domain but the corpus has nothing on it. Example: "What's the noise limit for drone deliveries in Pioneer Square?" The right response is "the corpus does not contain a rule on drone-delivery noise" and nothing else.
Out of domainwrong corpus
The question is outside Seattle regulatory text entirely. "What's the federal lead paint disclosure rule?" The answer points the user at the right authority instead of guessing.
False premisepremise check
The question assumes a fact that is wrong. "Since Seattle has no minimum stall size..." The model corrects the premise first, then answers the underlying question with citations.
05 · Latency & cost

What a question costs

Per-question operational numbers, measured at current scale. Latency depends mostly on retrieval and rerank; LLM synthesis is a relatively small fraction of total wall-clock time.

Per-question budgetv1 averages
Tier-1 lookupabout 1.5s · about $0.002
Single retrieval pass, no decomposition, short synthesis. Most of the latency is the LLM call itself.
Tier-3 cross-docabout 3s · about $0.005
Decomposition into 2-3 sub-queries, parallel retrieval, graph expansion, then synthesis.
Tier-5 conflictabout 6-9s · about $0.012
Conflict questions need both sides retrieved, both reranked, both fed in. Synthesis is longer because the answer explains the disagreement.
Refusalabout 0.8s · about $0.0005
When retrieval comes back empty or low-confidence, the synthesis prompt short-circuits to a refusal. Cheapest path through the pipeline.
