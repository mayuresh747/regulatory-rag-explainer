PolicyBridge Benchmark Summary

Comparison of four systems on regulatory numeric-answer and citation-grounding tasks.

Executive Summary

This benchmark compares four different systems across 35 questions. PolicyBridge was originally built on GPT 5.1. Because public ChatGPT evolved and the older public model could no longer be benchmarked directly, we tested the original PolicyBridge (GPT 5.1), a PolicyBridge variant using GPT 5.5, and two public GPT 5.5 variants.

The public GPT 5.5 comparison has two versions: one without PolicyBridge-style system instructions and one with the same formatting and refusal instructions used by the system. This separates raw public-model behavior from public-model behavior when given comparable answer-format and refusal guidance.

Hypothesis Questions

H1: Does PolicyBridge improve grounded regulatory answers? The RAG systems should cite and support answers more reliably than public GPT, especially public GPT without system instructions.

H2: Does GPT 5.5 improve PolicyBridge? PolicyBridge (GPT 5.5) should improve or stabilize numeric extraction compared with the original PolicyBridge (GPT 5.1).

H3: Do system instructions matter for public GPT 5.5? The public GPT 5.5 version with formatting and refusal instructions should be more disciplined than the no-instruction baseline.

H4: Where does complexity break performance? Higher-level, multi-source, conflict, and adversarial questions should reveal failures that simple direct lookups do not expose.

Question Creation Method

Because expert-written benchmark questions were not available, LLMs were used to draft candidate questions. Gemini 3.1 Pro and Opus 4.7 were given access to limited regulatory documents, for example SMC Chapter 23, and asked to form numeric-answerable queries and identify where the answers were located. The final user-facing questions avoided giving away the section or citation path. After generation, the answers and required source locations were manually verified against the cited sections.

L1 - Single section, one agency

The answer is a direct numeric lookup from one section in one document from one agency.

L2 - Multiple sections, same agency document

The answer combines values from multiple sections in the same document from the same agency.

L3 - Multiple documents, same agency

The answer combines information spread across different documents from one agency.

L4 - Multiple agencies, complementary rules

The answer combines sections from different agencies where the rules are complementary rather than conflicting.

L5 - Cross-agency conflict

Two agencies or authorities provide different numeric rules for the same topic, so the answer must surface the tension or controlling value.

L6 - Adversarial / false premise

The question contains a trap such as a planted wrong number, nonexistent provision, misleading citation, or baited computation.

Conclusion & Takeaways

No single system wins every dimension. GPT 5.5 with PolicyBridge-style system instructions has the best available numeric correctness, but PolicyBridge (GPT 5.1) has the strongest evidence-support score. That means the most fluent or numerically accurate answer is not always the most reliable regulatory answer.

System instructions help public GPT, but they do not fully replace retrieval. The public GPT 5.5 system with instructions performs much better than the no-instruction baseline on evidence support, especially in multi-agency and conflict levels. The no-instruction baseline can still guess or recall many numbers, but it often fails the citation and source-grounding bar.

PolicyBridge (GPT 5.5) improves some numeric behavior but loses some grounding. It improves over PolicyBridge (GPT 5.1) on Exp1a-1, including harder L6 numeric cases, but it trails PolicyBridge (GPT 5.1) on Exp1b. This suggests the GPT 5.5 upgrade should be evaluated with retrieval, citation formatting, and refusal behavior tuned together rather than treated as a drop-in replacement.

The hardest failures are not simple lookups. L1 and L2 questions are comparatively stable. L5 and L6 expose the real product risk: conflicting authorities, false premises, missing provisions, and answers that sound correct but are attached to weak or wrong evidence.
