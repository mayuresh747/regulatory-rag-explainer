  Seattle Regulatory RAG · Methodology

Benchmark methodology

A 120-question evaluation of the Seattle Regulatory RAG system across two regulatory domains, Parking and Fire Code, stratified into six difficulty levels (L1–L6). Each entry is manually verified against the source PDF before scoring.

120 verified questions · 2 topics · Parking, Fire Code · 6 difficulty levels · 3 systems compared
01 · The Premise

Why this benchmark exists

Eight regulatory bodies govern construction and land use in Seattle: the Washington Administrative Code (WAC), the Revised Code of Washington (RCW), the Seattle Municipal Code (SMC), Director's Rules, IBC-WA, Washington court opinions, Governor's orders, and SPU Design Standards. These bodies cross-reference one another, occasionally contradict one another, and periodically renumber their own sections.

In this context a language model can produce a confidently worded answer that cites a provision repealed years earlier. No public benchmark covers Seattle-specific regulatory retrieval. Because the system under evaluation is a customized retrieval-augmented agent over a local corpus, an in-house benchmark is required to assess its factual accuracy, citation fidelity, and refusal behavior against ground truth derived from the original source PDFs.

What the benchmark coversscope
In scope · six measured metrics
  • M1 · Citation precision: do the cited sections resolve in the corpus?
  • M2 · Citation recall: are the gold-required sections actually cited?
  • M3 · Source attribution: does the prose name the right issuing body (SMC, WAC, RCW, IBC-WA)?
  • Critical hallucination rate: rows where every cited section fails to resolve.
  • M4 · Correctness (LLM-judged 0–5): semantic equivalence to the gold answer.
  • Faithfulness (LLM-judged 0–5): claims supported by the system's own cited sources.
Out of scope
User-interface polish, end-to-end latency, conversational follow-up, multi-turn behavior, and any property beyond the truth of the answer and its citations.
02 · The Gold Set

How questions are authored

Question authoring follows a two-stage process. A large language model (Gemini 3.1 Pro) is first prompted with a regulatory section and asked to propose candidate questions whose answer is a number, list, or short phrase appearing literally in the text. The model returns each candidate together with a verbatim source quote, the source document, and a page number.

A human reviewer then opens the original PDF, navigates to the cited page, and matches the proposed quote against the text character-for-character. A question enters the gold set only when the quote is verbatim and the answer is correctly derived from its surrounding context. The source PDF is the only authority; no question enters the benchmark on the model's word alone.

The verification chain0 / 5 stages
1
Candidate generation (Gemini 3.1 Pro)
For each section in the Parking and Fire Code corpora, Gemini 3.1 Pro proposes 3–5 candidate questions whose answer is a literal value or short phrase in the text. The model also returns the quoted source sentence and the page number.
2
Open the original PDF
The reviewer navigates to the cited page in the source document. Paraphrases and database snapshots are not accepted.
3
Match the quoted sentence verbatim
The proposed quote is searched on the cited page. If it is not present verbatim, the candidate is rejected. OCR drift and paraphrase are caught at this step.
4
Validate the surrounding context
The reviewer confirms that the answer follows from the cited sentence and its immediate context, rather than from an adjacent paragraph, header, or misread merged cell.
5
Record the verbatim quote, document, and page
The exact sentence, source document, and page number are recorded against the question. The entry is now admitted to the gold set.
A question is a candidate, not gold.

Candidates that fail any verification step are reformulated, deferred, or retained as no-answer adversarial items. The final gold set contains 60 verified questions per topic, 10 at each of six difficulty levels, yielding 120 entries in total.

03 · Difficulty Levels

Six levels, ten questions each per topic

Each question is assigned to one of six difficulty levels, L1 through L6. The set is balanced by construction: 10 questions per level for each of the two topics, yielding 60 questions per topic and 120 entries in total. Reporting is stratified by level so that strong performance on basic lookups does not conceal weak performance on adversarial or cross-agency reasoning.

04 · What We Measure · Round 2 Results

Six metrics across three systems

Six metrics are computed over the 120-question gold set (10 questions per topic per level, totalling 20 per level across Parking and Fire Code) for three systems: Arm A · KG-RAG (ours), Arm C · Naive RAG (vector-only retrieval, no graph), and Arm D · GPT-5.1 (no retrieval). Four metrics are deterministic Python scorers; two are LLM-judged by Claude Opus 4.7 at temperature zero, cross-family from the synthesizer to avoid same-model bias.

On rows like this, M2 is partly measuring whose prose style happens to echo the question's syntax, not whose retrieval found the right provision.
05 · Defending an Answer

The provenance of one entry

Every entry in the gold set is auditable. Five attributes are recorded at verification time: the question's stated intent, the cited authority (section identifier, document, and page), the verbatim source text, the verification record describing how the entry was checked against the source PDF, and a verdict tag that captures its difficulty level and any adversarial framing.

An entry can be revised only by producing either (a) a different verbatim quote from the same PDF page, or (b) a later ordinance that amends the cited provision. Reformulation, paraphrase, or expert opinion is not sufficient to overturn an entry.

The benchmark is a research instrument, not a marketing artifact.

Round 2 results are reported above for the full 120-question gold set across Arms A, C, and D. They are presented exactly as the scoring pipeline returned them, including the metrics where the system under evaluation lags. The point of the benchmark is to surface where the system fails, not to produce a high score. Any gold entry that cannot be defended against the source PDF is rewritten or removed before scoring.
