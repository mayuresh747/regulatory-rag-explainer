Chunking and embedding

How the regulatory corpus is cut into chunks and turned into vectors. What we use, what we considered, what we rejected, with side-by-side examples.

8 per-agency chunkers · text-embedding-3-large · 3072 d · Markdown for tables
01 · Why It Matters

Why this matters

Two upstream choices decide most of a RAG system's quality. Chunking sets what slice of text the model sees. Embedding sets which slice the system can find. If a chunk boundary lands mid-clause, the model sees a half-rule. If the embedding model can't tell paraphrases apart, the right chunk never gets retrieved.

This page walks through both choices: what we use, what we considered, what we rejected, and what the differences look like on real documents from the corpus.

02 · The Chunking Space

Chunking strategies

Four common families:

The four familiestrade-off space
Fixed-sizenaive baseline
WhatSplit every N tokens. Simple, fast, deterministic. Used by most "RAG starter" tutorials.
Recursive character splitterlangchain default
WhatTry splitting on paragraphs first, then sentences, then words. Falls back gracefully but ignores document structure.
Semantic (semchunk, Chonkie)embedding-based
WhatUse embedding similarity between adjacent sentences to find natural breakpoints. Good for prose, blind to legal section structure.
Structure-aware, hand-builtours
WhatOne chunker per document family. Each knows the agency's section header format, citation regex, table conventions, and ordinance-history boilerplate.

None of the generic families respect the structure of an SMC section, a WAC subsection, or a court opinion's FACTS / ANALYSIS / CONCLUSION headings. We built one chunker per document family.

03 · What We Picked

What we use: eight per-agency chunkers

Each agency in the corpus has its own self-contained chunker in src/chunkers/. None inherits from a generic splitter. Each one knows its document family's section header format, citation regex, table conventions, and boilerplate to strip.

The eight chunkerssrc/chunkers/
SMC2000 tok cap
Cuts onXX.YY.ZZZ   Title headers. Sub-chunks at A./B. subsection markers. Strips ordinance history footers. Detects definitions ("X" means …) and tables.
WAC2000 tok cap
Cuts onWAC 365-196-875 identifiers plus title. Knows about cross-references to RCW chapters.
RCW2000 tok cap
Cuts onRCW 36.70A.535 identifiers. Tracks effective dates and amendment history per section.
Director's Rules1500 tok cap
Cuts onDR identifier (DR 8-2003), then on numbered policies. Tighter cap because DRs are interpretive and topically dense.
Court opinions3000 tok cap
Cuts onALL-CAPS headings (FACTS, ANALYSIS, CONCLUSION). Drops slip-opinion notices. Tags concurrence and dissent. Larger cap for narrative reasoning.
IBC-WA2000 tok cap
Cuts onBuilding-code chapter and section headers. Special handling for WA amendments overlaid on the IBC.
SPU Standards2000 tok cap
Cuts onDesign standard chapters and tables (stormwater triggers, side-sewer specs). Tables are the most valuable content.
Governor Orders2000 tok cap
Cuts onOrder number and section. Captures effective and rescinded dates so superseded orders don't get treated as active.

Common spine: regex first, sub-chunk if too long, semchunk as a last-resort fallback. Tables get extracted separately as Markdown. Every chunk carries metadata: citation, section title, agency, content type, table type if relevant.

06 · The Embedding Space

Embedding model choice

An embedding turns a chunk into a vector. The model decides whether two paraphrases land near each other in vector space. For regulatory text this matters constantly: a user asks "max height in lowrise", the corpus says "maximum structure height in LR1 zones".

The options we evaluated:

Embedding options we considered2025
text-embedding-3-small1536 d · cheaper
NoteSame price tier per token. Lower dimensionality misses fine-grained legal nuance: paraphrased terms ("setback" vs "minimum yard depth") embed less reliably. 6-8% lower retrieval accuracy on legal QA.
text-embedding-3-largeours · 3072 d
NoteThe expensive vectors aren't expensive. 3072 dimensions capture the legal nuance we need. $0.13 per 1M tokens. The cost premium over -small is rounding error at our corpus size.
Vision embeddings for tablesrejected
Note10× the cost, 10× the latency, lower accuracy than Markdown on structured tables. 2025 research is consistent: regulatory tables embed better as text.
Multilingual e5-largefuture
NoteSelf-hostable, no API. We'd consider this when the corpus expands to Spanish, Vietnamese, Chinese, Somali queries. Not v1.
Voyage law-2rejected v1
NoteDomain-tuned for legal but trained on US case law, not state administrative code. Marginal accuracy gain didn't justify the API switch.

08 · The Table Problem

09 · How We Got Here

Decision sequence

Each choice constrained the next. Listed in the order they were made.

The chaindecision log
1 · Use real PDFs as ground truth
WhyDocling preserves table structure better than alternatives. Every chunk traces to a page in the original PDF. No paraphrase, no model-summarized intermediate.
2 · Chunk per agency, not per project
WhySMC sections and court opinions don't look anything alike. One generic chunker would lose at both. Eight chunkers cost more to build, win on every doc.
3 · Keep tables as Markdown, not vision
WhyVision embeddings are slower, costlier, and less accurate on structured tables in 2025. Markdown preserves rows and columns; the LLM reads it natively.
4 · Cap chunk size per doc type
WhySMC sections fit in 2000 tokens, Director's Rules need 1500, court opinions sometimes need 3000 for one ANALYSIS subsection. One cap doesn't fit all.
5 · Embed with text-embedding-3-large
Why3072 dimensions capture legal nuance the small model misses. Cost premium is negligible at corpus scale (~$200 total for first ingestion).
6 · Store in LanceDB with hybrid search
WhyVector + BM25 fused. Hybrid lifts retrieval on numeric and exact-section-number queries that vector alone misses. Embedded DB, no separate server.
